Basic Electronics

Part 1 Introduction to Electronics

1.1 Matter

  • Occupies space
  • Has weight

1.2 Elements

  • Basic building block of nature
  • Can't be reduced to a simpler substance by chemical means
  • Over 100 known elements

1.3 Parts of an atom

  • Nucleus - located at center of atom
  • Proton - positively charged particles inside nucleus
  • Neutrons - uncharged particles inside nucleus
  • Electrons - negatively charged particles that orbit nucleus

1.4 Properties of atom

  • Atomic number - the number of protons in the nuclues of the atom
  • Atomic weight - the mass of an atom determined by the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus

1.5 Elecctron Properties

  • Shell - where the electrons orbit around the nucleus in a specific sequence
  • Valence Shell - the outer most shell
  • Valence - the number of electrons contained in the valence shell

1.6 Coductors

  • Materials that contain a large number of free electrons (3 or fewer in valence shell)

1.7 Insulators

  • Prevent the flow of electricity
  • Stabilized by absorbing valence electrons
  • Insulators contain five or more electrons in the valence shell

1.8 Semiconductors

  • Can be altered to function as either conductor or insulator
  • Negative ion - a negatively charged atom
  • Positive ion - a positively charged atom
  • Semiconductors have four electrons in the valence shell

1.9 Ionization

  • The process of gaining or losing electrons and is a significant player in current flow

1.10 Current

  • Current - movement of electrons from negatively charged atoms to positively charged atoms, it is usually represented as I
  • Coulomb - also represented as C, it has 6.24 x 1018 electrons

1.11 Ampere

  • One C moving past a single point in one second
  • Also represented by A
  • Named after French physicisy Andre Marie Ampere
  • Current is measured in amperes

1.12 Potential

  • Potential - The ability of the source to perform electrical work
  • Difference of Potential - causes electrons to move or flow in a circuit and is known commonly as electromotive force (emf) or voltage

1.13 Voltage

  • The force that moves the electrons in the circuit
  • The pressure or pump that moves electrons
  • represented as E
  • Unit of measure called the volt

1.14 Resistance

  • Opposition to the flow of electrons
  • Degree of resistance depends on the size, shape or temperature
  • Unit of measure called Ohms (Ω)
  • Conductors - have low resistnce to current flow with many free electrons
  • Insulators - have high resistnce to current flow with few free electrons

Summary

  • Let's begin on the atomic level and then slowly work are way up while trying to understand the important physical properties that allow us to create amazing new technologies. What is a thing? A thing, in the most scientific view, is matter. If it occupies space and has weight, then it is matter which makes it a thing.

  • Next question, what is matter made of? That is what we call the elements. The basic building blocks of nature, Elements can't be reduced to a simpler substance by chemical means, and there are amazingly only 100 known elements that make up the etire universe.

  • So now let's quickley break down what makes an element a specific element and get to the first important piece of electricity. So there's these things called atoms. Atoms contain a nucleus that is located in the center of the atom. Inside every nuclues there are protons and neutrons, and the protons have something that is known as a positive charge while the nuetrons have no charge. It is this charge that holds the universe together. But most atoms have one more part which is what is most important and fundamental to know, the electron. A negatively charged particle the orbits the proton and nuetron filled nucleus like a moon around a planet.

  • So now we have established what a element is and what an atom is, now what? Well we want to know what makes elements different from one another? Well an element is just a large number of atoms which all are identical, so to make a different element, get atoms that look different, and weigh different. This can be accomplished by finding atoms with a different number of protons in the nucleus and a different weight (remeber that to be matter, the thing must have a weight and take up space, even if it is really small). So we can classify elements by the composition of their atoms that make them up, namely atomic number and atomic weight.

  • The atomic number of an element is the number of protons in the nucleus of the atom. The atomic weight is the combined mass of the protons and nuetrons (nucleus). Electrons are not included in the mass as their weight is negligible

  • Thus far, we know how elements are composed of and we know elements make up everything. We now have a really solid base to begin talking about the properties of electrons. Going forward, the only thing we really care about as far as electronics go are electrons.

  • Electrons orbit the nucleus of an atom on a model that we call shells. Atoms have a different number of shells depending on how many electrons it has. As far as we are concerned, the only shell that matters is the one that orbits the furthest away from the nucleus known as the valence shell.

  • Elements that are made up of atoms that have few (three of less) electrons in their valence shell are also good conductors. What this mean is that they have the potential to get rid of their electrons and give them to another atom (allow flow of electricity).

  • Elements that have many electons in their valence shell (five or more) are likely to absorb electrons to fill the remaining spots in their valence shell.
  • This brings us to the third type of element, the element that has four electrons in it's valence shell. The most magical and important type of element in all of the technology created in this world so far. The semiconductor. A wonderful property that allows materials to be both conductors and insulators under certain conditions. What us clever humans have done is learn to control the conditions which make the semiconductors switch properties. This basically creates a switch we can control with current (don't worry we haven't gotten there yet) that is mere nano-meters in size. We will be going way more in depth into this later so don't worry.

What is current?

Charges

  • First law of electrostatic charges - like charges repel eachother
  • Second law of electrostatic charges - unlike charges repel eachother
  • Coulomb (C ) - unit for measuring charge, one coloumb is 6,240,000,000,000,000,000 electrons or 6.24 x 1018

Current

  • Current - the drift of electrons from an area of negative charge to an area of positive charge
  • Ampere (A) - The amount of current in a conductor when one coulomb of charge moves past a point in one second

First Relationship

  • The relationship between amperes and coulombs per second can be expressed as: $$I = \frac{Q}{t}$$
  • I = current measured in amperes
  • Q = quantity of electrical charge in coulombs
  • t = time in seconds

Hole

  • Hole - the movement of an electron from one atom to the next, creating the appearence of a positive charge moving in the opposite direction

Voltage source

  • Supplies electrons from one end of the conductors
  • Removes electrons from the other end of the conductor
  • Think of it like a pump

Scientific notation

  • Means of using single digit numbers plus powers of 10 to express large and small numbers $$3000 = 3 x 10^3$$ $$.0000003 = 10^{-6}$$

How many amperes?

  • Milliampere (mA) - used more frequently than ampere and is equal to $$\frac{1}{1000}A = .001A$$
  • Microampere (μA) - used more frequently than ampere and is equal to $$\frac{1}{1000000}A = .000001A$$
